Where Does PII Get Exposed — and Where Should We Focus?

"Harvest now, decrypt later" is the phrase you hear in every PQC conversation. But the more actionable question for a network engineer is: where exactly can an adversary capture your traffic, and what are they capturing at each point?

When a user submits a login, a payment, or any sensitive form — that data travels across two separate internet-exposed legs before it reaches your application:

Leg 1
Browser → CDN

User's device to the CDN edge (Cloudflare), across the public internet. Anyone who can observe traffic between the user and the CDN can capture this leg.

Leg 2
CDN → Origin

Cloudflare back across the internet to your origin — F5 BIG-IP or a cloud load balancer. A separate TLS session, separately vulnerable, and separately configurable.

There is also a third exposure path: trusted external systems — partner APIs, SaaS platforms, third-party integrations — communicating inbound to your origin directly over the internet, bypassing the CDN entirely. The diagram below maps where each harvest opportunity sits.

Why hardening the CDN and origin layers closes most of the risk: Both legs where PII crosses the internet — browser to CDN and CDN to origin — pass through TLS termination points you control and can configure. Secure both legs with a quantum-resistant key exchange (ML-KEM via TLS 1.3) and an adversary capturing everything in transit today cannot decrypt it, even with a future quantum computer. You don't need to solve every device or every endpoint at once. Close the two internet hops that carry PII, and you've eliminated the harvest window that matters most.

TLS 1.2 vs TLS 1.3

Why the TLS version actually matters here. TLS 1.3 made the key exchange pluggable. That's the architectural reason ML-KEM can exist at all. TLS 1.2 simply has no slot for it. And TLS 1.3 alone, without ML-KEM, still leaves you exposed.

✓ Verified against a Cloudflare-fronted domain (April 2026)

The command below confirms ML-KEM is actually being negotiated — not just theoretically supported. The key line to look for is Negotiated TLS1.3 group: X25519MLKEM768.

Requires OpenSSL 3.5+. See the OpenSSL setup guide if you hit errors. Running in Git Bash on Windows or building from source on WSL are both covered. 

LD_LIBRARY_PATH=/usr/local/openssl35/lib64 \
  /usr/local/openssl35/bin/openssl s_client \
  -connect <your-cloudflare-domain>:443 \
  -tls1_3 -groups "X25519MLKEM768:x25519" \
  -CAfile /etc/ssl/certs/ca-certificates.crt \
  -brief

Negotiated TLS1.3 group: X25519MLKEM768  ← this is what you want to see
